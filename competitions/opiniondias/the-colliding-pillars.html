The Colliding Pillars

  • By: Alok Kumar Mishra
    alokkm20@iitk.ac.in
    200096

Disclaimer: The views and the opinion expressed in this article are those of the author and does not necessarily reflect the views and positions of Public Policy and Opinion Cell IITK.


On October 16, 2015, the Hon'ble Supreme court using its power of Judicial Review, declared the NJAC (National Judicial Appointments Commission) against the Basic Structure of the Constitution and, therefore, null and void. Seven years have passed, and now the issue has risen again. The tussle between the Judiciary and the center is making headlines every day. Recently, the Hon’ble Supreme Court openly published the intelligence report which the center produced for three judge-candidates recommended by the Hon'ble SC. This has made things worse. What is the way out of this feud?


The main reason behind this quarrel is the appointment method of Judges in the Supreme and High court. The Hon'ble SC opined in the "Third Judges Case 1998" that a Collegium of 5 Judges ( CJI and 4 Senior Most Judges of Hon'ble SC) will recommend names to the center. But over the years, the collegium's transparency and unbiasedness have been questioned again and again. Judges are likely to have some bias towards the jurist with whom they have worked ( e.g., a Judge from the parent HC) or have seen them work (e.g., advocates). At times, accusations of nepotism have also been made. In 2015, The center said that the collegium is inefficient, has selected non deserving Judges and needs to change.


The NJAC was one such attempt to revamp the appointment process. It provided a committee consisting of the CJI, two other senior judges of the Hon’ble SC, the Law minister, and two eminent personalities chosen by the CJI, the PM, and the opposition leader. The judges were to be selected by this committee, by an absolute majority of 5/6 members. I feel that there are indeed some serious issues with this committee that endanger the independence of the Judiciary. Out of the six members, the Law minister was from the ruling party, and there are humongous chances that at least one of the two eminent persons would also favor the ruling party. Since the approval of at least five was required for a decision, the ruling party would have an absolute veto in the appointment of Judges.This would have been a massive blow to the independence of the Judiciary.


We have already seen in the tenure of Smt. Indira Gandhi when the Judiciary nearly came under the direct control of the PM. The candidates favored by the PM were made the CJI after senior and deserving judges' resignations. Justice A.N Ray was made the CJI superseding Justice Shelat, Grover and Hegde. Later Justice MH Beg superseded Justice HR Khanna. Both Ray and Beg had opined in the favor of the Government in the Kesavananda Bharati case, 1973. The decisions taken by the court during the emergency also reflected the center's dominance over the Judiciary.


Therefore the collegium and the NJAC have their own serious problems. I feel that currently, both the Hon’ble SC and the Center are worsening the situation by quarreling and denigrating one another. Instead of all this, the two pillars should sit and discuss a way out of this endless cycle of accusation. The need of the hour is a body similar to the NJAC, which does not give the veto to the center and also enhances the level of transparency and unbiasedness of the appointment process. This will not only end this tussle and the allegations but will also strengthen the citizens' trust in the Judiciary and the cente
