India’s Highest Population, An Asset?...

  • By: Vijaya Mishra

Disclaimer: The views and the opinion expressed in this article are those of the author and does not necessarily reflect the views and positions of Public Policy and Opinion Cell IITK.


According to a Non-Governmental Organization, the World Population Review, India’s population at the end of 2022 has surpassed China’s, becoming the most populous country on the planet. In such a scenario, it’s natural to ask whether the highest population is an asset or not.


Demographic Dividend and Population as a Boon: The United Nations Population Fund defines Demography Dividend is the economic growth potential that can result from shifts in a population’s age structure, mainly when the share of the working-age population (15 to 64) is larger than the non-working-age share of the population. China was able to reap the benefits of demography dividend with its successful industrial and investment policy to achieve phenomenal growth from 1960 till recent years. Apart from the demography dividend, a huge population also offers a great market potential attracting investments also. It also acts as a cushion during the time when global demand is uncertain.


Population is not the root cause of evil, but inequality is. We can avail a minimum living standard to our large population by addressing the huge income and wealth inequality. According to inequality report 2023, published by Oxfam, richest 1% of Indian population own more than 40% of Indian wealth while bottom 50% share just 3%. Often population is considered a burden with the notion that it depletes resources and causes environmental damages, but if we dive deeper, we will find that population is not the root cause of evil, but inequality is.



Source: World Inequality Report 2022

Way Forward: India’s 65% population below the age of 35, waiting for the Demography Dividend to be reaped. Building on Human Capital (Health and Education), employment generation, inclusive migrant policy and ensuring social security to its migrant population are necessities. Also taxing super-rich is a way forward.
