UP Population Bill, 2021

  • By: Aditya K. Rao
  • Sunday, 11 July 2021

The Uttar Pradesh State Law Commission has put out the draft of THE UTTAR PRADESH POPULATION(CONTROL, STABILISATION AND WELFARE) BILL, 2021 for public comments and recommendations a couple of days before the World Population Day, being celebrated today. The state is second in line to introduce such a bill on population control after Assam did so in June 2021. Such a Bill (Population Regulation Bill, 2019) was even proposed in Rajya Sabha in July 20219 by Rakesh Sinha.

In the country with the second-largest population, the demands of such population control measures have been up for discussion for several decades. The government initiated several family planning schemes, which have had varied consequences

Understanding the need for Population Control:

From the 1970s to 2010s, the 10-year growth rate has gradually come down to 16.8% from as high as 25.9%.

However, the share of India in the global population has been strictly increasing. We currently account for 17.8% of global population but only 2.4% of global surface and 4% of water resources (2011 data). According to estimates by UN World Population Prospects 2019, our population will have a continuous positive growth rate till 2060, stagnating our population at 1.65 billion until it starts decreasing.

The general consensus is that our current problems of poverty, unemployment, low per capita income and related issues are largely because of overpopulation. Often our large population is termed as a large and active workforce. But that is only when there is a significant investment in pre-requirements like education, healthcare and nutrition. With such a large population, and a significant proportion of it ageing under 50, the pressure to meet their basic requirements of quality education, healthcare, nutrition and employment in the future will be huge when even the existing system is not working well in the current times. This crisis will only deepen with the increasing population because of the finiteness of our resources. The Census of India states that the percentage of the urban population has also been increasing and hence the pressure will be more on the urban economy.

Lessons from History

The initial measures of population control mainly were calling public for family planning and promotion to contraceptives till the 1960s. The ineffectiveness of these measures forced the government to frame more target-oriented policies and use coercive methods to implement population control. During the 1970s, to bring down the annual growth rate to 2.5%, sterilisation camps were thought of as an effective solution for population control and family planning. The use of force to promote this campaign led to widespread fear among the masses and ultimately to the failure of the program as the growth rate increased significantly

Post-1980s, the government adopted voluntary family planning schemes. The effectiveness of voluntary measures without significant incentives is questionable since the growth remained significantly higher than in other countries. The overall reduction in population growth rate is more likely caused because of increased literacy and socio-economic development.

Brief Outline of UP Population Bill, 2021

Unlike its Chinese counterpart, the Bill drives to achieve its aim by incentivising having less than three children rather than criminalising its violation. The following benefits are given to public servants if they voluntarily undergo sterilisation upon themselves or their spouse:

  1. Two additional increments during entire services.
  2. Subsidy and loans for housing purchase and construction.
  3. Maternity/paternity leave for 12 months with full salary.
  4. Free healthcare benefits and insurance coverage of the child till the age of 20.
  5. Preference to admission to educational institutes and government jobs.
  6. Scholarship to the girl child

Families living below the poverty line will get added monetary benefits in case of having a single child. These incentives (barring a few) are extended to the general public (non-public sector) if they undergo voluntary sterilisation. It would be interesting to see how the private sector reacts to such a law and if it initiates any such measures in compliance with the above Bill.

Moreover, the Bill places a few restrictions and disincentives on families violating the two-child policy.

  1. Disallow contesting any local-body elections.
  2. Limit ration card units up to four.
  3. Debarring from Government-sponsored welfare schemes or subsidies
  4. Bar on applying and promotion in government jobs

Do we require a national law?

Probably not.

The TFR of most states is below 2.1. Only populated states like Bihar (TFR-3.0), UP (TFR-3.0), Madhya Pradesh (TFR-2.7) and Rajasthan(TFR-2.7) have such high numbers (Data of 2017; not shown in the chart as data of 2019 unavailable). The large population size of these states push the national average of TFR above 2.2 .

Prolonged TFR less than 2.1 will lead to a significantly high number of elderly people a couple of decades from now. And we will be exactly where Japan and China are today. Instead, a decentralised population control policy in states of higher population and TFR will prove more effective in mitigating impacts of a large population without long term ill-effects

Role of Women

An important consideration while chalking out a population control policy is the strengthening of women in family planning. Several studies show that higher levels of education and income amongst women increase the mean age of marriage and decrease the fertility rate.

As the National Family Health Survey-4 (2015-16) notes, women in the lowest wealth quintile have an average of 1.6 more children than women in the highest wealth quintile, translating to a total fertility rate of 3.2 children versus 1.5 children moving from the wealthiest to the poorest. Similarly, the number of children per woman declines with a woman’s level of schooling. Women with no schooling have an average of 3.1 children, compared with 1.7 children for women with 12 or more years of schooling.

This reveals the depth of the connections between health, education and inequality, with those having little access to health and education being caught in a cycle of poverty, leading to more and more children, and the burden that state control on the number of children could impose on the weakest. Hence to achieve a fertility rate of 2.1, designing a policy that focuses on giving the control of family planning to women by directing incentives towards them can be more fruitful in the long run to control the population.

This, however, is not the case with the current Bill. Incentivising the employed member of the family may lessen the desired impact due to lesser female employment. The future schemes under this Bill will have to include women as target beneficiaries for better results

Conclusion

Implementing such law in specific states will bring us down from the projected 1.65 billion population by 2060. If we can stagnate this number to 1.40-1.45 billion within next two decades, we will be at a great vantage point. We need to simultaneously prepare to cater for the needs of a population of 1.40-1.45 billion as this number is inevitable.
