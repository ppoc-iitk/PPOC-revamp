Lack of Health and Hygiene Awareness for Women in Rural India.

  • By: Jonathan Doe

  • MarketingManagement

Women in rural India have been living in dire lack of sanitation and adequate health facilities. When tied with other elementary facilities like education and hygiene awareness, the condition is unspeakably distorted. The 2011 United Nations Development Programme's Human Development Report ranked India 132 out of 187 in terms of gender inequality. More than 52% of Indian rural women dwell in insufficiency of menstrual sanitation, post-natal care, sex education and more.

The percentage of rural women participating in forced labor due to unemployment in India is imminently high in comparison to their urban counterparts.

Lack of hygiene and health facilities

According to a report by IMS Institute for Healthcare Informatics, in rural areas, to seek OPD treatment 32 per cent of rural respondents had to travel over 5 kms while in urban areas only 8% of the total population has to travel more than 5 kms to get basic health aid. The difference seems much bigger when we consider the percentage difference of rural to urban population in India to be around 72%-28%.

Analysis Methodology

In this article we will analyze the current demographics of women’s healthcare and hygiene in rural India based on currently available information and predictions on the following objectives:

  • Addressing healthcare concerns of women in rural India and comparing the to facilities available in the urban counterparts.
  • Analyzing the menstrual health and sexual health scenario of women in rural India.
  • Healthcare awareness and government schemes.

Analysis

According to research by the SmileFoundation India, “75% of the healthcare infrastructure is concentrated in urban areas where only 27% of the total Indian population is living” “According to a KMPG report, “74% of Indian doctors are catering to the needs of the urban population.”

The Ministry of Health and Family Welfare has introduced a scheme for promotion of menstrual hygiene among adolescent girls in the age group of 10-19 year in rural areas. The major objectives of the scheme are:

  1. To increase awareness among adolescent girls on Menstrual Hygiene
  2. To increase access to and use of high-quality sanitary napkins to adolescent girls in rural areas.
  3. To ensure safe disposal of Sanitary Napkins in an environmentally friendly manner.

Government Schemes for rural women welfare in India:

  • Janani Suraksha Yojna
  • Janani Shishu Suraksha Karyakram
  • Dakshata Implementation Package
  • Pradhanmantri Surakshit Matritva Abhiyan
  • Menstrual Hygiene Scheme
  • Kasturba Poshan Sahay Yojna

Conclusion

Even though the status of women’s health and hygiene infrastructure in India has largely improved in the past few decades, the condition remains far from ideal as a large proportion of Indian women, especially from rural and tribal regions suffer lack of fundamental healthcare and gender specific treatment infrastructure. The stigma associated with girl childbirth and preference of boy child in rural India has made sex-selective abortion one of the most prealent health crime against Indian women. Other health hazards such as cardiovascular diseases, HIV/AIDS, maternal health, malnutrition, breast cancer, UTIs etc and socio-human issues like lack of education, domestic violence, cultural backwardness have prevented women from gaining adequate awareness of their rights and benefit from government schemes.

Resources such as midwives, qualified doctors, or ambulances are not readily available at all hospitals; especially in rural India.It is important to understand the present need of health infrastructure in rural India and revolutionize a more comprehensive reform system in healthcare with proper reservation for maternal care and women welfare.
