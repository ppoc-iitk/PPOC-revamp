The British Revolving Door

  • By: Rituraj Sharma, Vrinda Sharma, Abhishek Punia, Vaishnavi Singh

Disclaimer: The views and the opinion expressed in this article are those of the author and does not necessarily reflect the views and positions of Public Policy and Opinion Cell IITK.

Rishi Sunak was sworn in as the first Asian British prime minister of the United Kingdom on 24 October 2022. In an unprecedented revolving door leading to Downing Street ( Residence & office of the PM), Sunak is the fifth PM Britain has seen in the last six years. Political instability, inflation, environmental hazards, energy crisis, probable bifurcation — nothing seems to be going right for the once great Britain. Let's articulate the ingredients for such a mess in a developed country and, towards the end, contemplate how our country has many of those still standing 'intact.'

It all started on 1 January 1973, when the UK became a member of the European Union( then EC ) under the leadership of Edward Heath, a Conservative party leader. Ironically enough, it was the Tories (A colloquial term used for the Conservative party leaders) who rallied the UK out of the Union, too, on 31 January 2021 under Boris Johnson. As is evident from the preceding statement, there was always volatility in public and leadership opinions on the position of the UK in the EU. Though, this was only truly realized in the 2016 referendum:- "Should the United Kingdom remain a member of the European Union or leave the European Union?".
The then Prime Minister of the UK, David Cameron, staked his PM chair on 'stay.' In the referendum, 51.89% voted in favor of leaving the EU (Leave), and 48.11% voted in favor of remaining a member of the EU (Remain). An outrageous verdict laid out the basis for a troublesome political scenario.

Cameron resigned soon after, remarking on the need and utility of the EU for Britain in his last speech at the podium of Number 10 ( Residence of the PM ). Theresa May, the second female prime minister sworn in. Touted to be indecisive and confused, she needed help to negotiate a formal approvable deal for Brexit in the Parliament. In an ugly position, she was to balance out the moderates and fundamentalists in the Parliament. The predicament was real — a hard Brexit or not? Her resilience had a number; after three rejections in the Parliament, she resigned in 2019.

Brexit, the number one priority of the Tory manifesto, led them to be victorious in the 2019 general elections. Boris Johnson was sworn in, and after two years of negotiations and drafting, proposed a Brexit deal that was accepted by all, and finally, Britain exited. While this was expected to be the closure of all woes for the Britons, Johnson's portfolio had humanitarian stains. Partygate scandals, sexual assault allegations against his ministers(Chris Pincher), party-before-country mentality (Owen Peterson scandal), and rising inflation — his simmering scandalous cauldron soon boiled up, and he resigned after being unable to hold together his cabinet as the ministers resigned in unprecedented numbers.

Then comes Liz Truss, Britain's least favorite, shortest-serving prime minister. She bestowed Britain with her radical "bold" financial policies in her leadership. Supposedly the only thing Britain did not need. She introduced tax cuts worth 45 billion pounds, the largest ever, with the plan to promote investment. Further, she planned on borrowing at least 60 billion pounds to finance a plan to cap high energy prices for households and businesses. This master plan would basically peak the already sky-high debts of the country. Despite contentions, she went ahead with the plan. Voila! As a result, the pound (UK's currency) fell drastically. It was valued at $1.03, the record lowest against the US dollar. Dysfunction in the market continued as she refrained from withdrawing. However, the pressure from the opposition forced her to repeal the policies, the key highlight of her 45-day stint as a prime minister.

While the whole world anticipates the next staggering turn of events in this story, all Britain wants is a boring prime minister who goes by the book and leads them out of this unprecedented delusional disorder.
Despite intense political turbulence, Britain exhibits signs of a healthy democracy. A democracy where leaders rule for the people, not for their lust for power; where ministers can oppose their prime minister; where a healthy opposition and media check the government; and where ministers are sacked for lack of ethics and discipline. In no way is the present UK a model to look up to, but indeed these conclusions can help us reflect on our country and contemplate the hypocrisy of the 'normalcy' in our political system.
